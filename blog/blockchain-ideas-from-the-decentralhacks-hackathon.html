4 Brilliant Blockchain Ideas from the DecentralHacks Hackathon

From blockchain-based voting to decentralised blood banks — four standout ideas from the 600+ developers who competed at the DecentralHacks hackathon.

Earlier this year, Octaloop conducted India's largest blockchain-based hackathon as part of The Road to Metamorphosis II — a virtual summit featuring talks and discussions from prominent members of the cryptocurrency community.

After a week-long test of skill and determination, mentor sessions with veteran blockchain developers, and many bug fixes, hackathon participants presented some truly innovative concepts in distributed ledger technology. Building on networks like Ethereum, Harmony, and Elastos, the contestants — many of whom were new to the space — were able to harness the power of blockchain and develop applications unlike anything we'd seen before.

ElectroCrypt

From a team that goes by the name GenesisX comes a blockchain-based voting system that integrates with the cloud to provide security and stability advantages over current solutions. Anjum Rashid, Temitayo Daniel, Gaurav Tiwari, and Ricardo Prins designed ElectroCrypt with the end-user in mind.

According to the team's brief, they created this app to be accessible to anyone, regardless of their technical knowledge. It also doesn't require a cryptocurrency wallet to pay transaction fees or cast votes — it registers ballot votes on behalf of the election organiser.

Built on the Matic network, a hybrid proof-of-stake and plasma-enabled Ethereum sidechain, the application creates a bridge to the decentralised Matic blockchain, where users can cast votes secured by cryptographic signatures.

DaanVeer

Just one individual made this decentralised crowdfunding application — and while it's only a proof of concept, it's a beautiful concept indeed. The app was inspired by the 2020 India Giving report from Charities Aid Foundation India, which states that 84% of individuals took part in at least one charitable activity in the last year.

However, the report also found that 52% of people thought a lack of transparency hindered NGO donations. DaanVeer, built at the hackathon by Shivam Agrawal on the Ethereum blockchain, aims to encourage people to donate through immutable and transparent transactions.

Charity organisations can register on the application and describe the cause for which they are raising funds. People can send money directly to these causes and see where and how the causes benefit from it. In a world where people prefer to donate directly to beneficiaries, an application like this could be a game-changer.

Blood Bank on Blockchain

Blockchain is gearing up to revolutionise the healthcare industry, especially in handling, storing, and managing medical records. Due to COVID-19, doctors must often diagnose patients remotely, and the ability to securely access relevant records is crucial.

This hackathon application, proposed by Gokul Alex for the Harmony blockchain, utilises ideas from his Semiott protocol to create a decentralised blood bank. The application harnesses the scalability and security of the Harmony protocol and implements privacy-preserving smart contracts on Ethereum.

According to the application brief, Gokul and his team are in talks with various blood banks and hospitals to deploy their future platform.

DSOC

Continuing on the theme of healthcare, the Decentralised Storage of Cases (DSOC) is a project designed to provide accurate information on the number of COVID-19 cases worldwide. Countries can — and have — misreported data on the number of infected people, and centralised systems perpetuate this manipulation. DSOC looks to eliminate inaccuracies by decentralising information sources.

The two team members behind the application — Prajwal Anagani and Aditya Upadhyay — claim it is unlike any other record-keeping blockchain, as it is simple and function-specific. The app only records the necessary data: the patient's name, ID, and test result. This could enable healthcare providers to contribute and update case data in real time, removing the onus of accuracy from a centrally controlled body.

Promoting Innovation

Despite the potential for blockchain to fundamentally change the workings of many industries worldwide, the technology is still mostly used in the financial services sphere. Hackathons like DecentralHacks present us with the unique opportunity to incentivise developers to test the waters of blockchain development — and with a prize pool of $15,000, even experienced programmers take notice.

At Octaloop, our goal is to foster a thriving community of people passionate about cryptocurrencies and blockchain technology. These programming marathons help bring more developers into the industry by spreading awareness and sparking interest in the younger crowd of developers that have yet to find a niche.

Blockchain is a very developer-centric field, and if we don't keep providing avenues to solve the industry's problems, interest and innovation will slowly fizzle out. A challenge is never easy — it's part of the definition — but solving them provides a sense of satisfaction that isn't reproducible in other scenarios. The creativity on display at a blockchain hackathon doesn't just provoke thought — it inspires action.
